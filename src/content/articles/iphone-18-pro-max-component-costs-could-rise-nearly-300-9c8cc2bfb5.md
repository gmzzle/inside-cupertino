The iPhone 18 Pro Max's bill of materials is set to jump nearly $300 compared to this year's model, according to a [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/10/iphone-18-pro-max-costs-could-rise/) report on Counterpoint Research estimates. The 1TB configuration is getting hit especially hard: NAND flash alone could run Apple north of $250, which is roughly what *all* the components in an iPhone 17 Pro Max cost combined. DRAM pricing is spiking too, both caught up in the memory shortage driven by AI server demand.

TSMC's 2nm process transition is the other big culprit. The A20 Pro will reportedly be Apple's first N2 chip, and wafer costs on bleeding-edge nodes always carry a premium. Early yields are typically rough too, which means more wasted silicon per good chip. Apple's been here before—the A17 Pro's N3 ramp wasn't cheap either—but this time they're dealing with memory inflation on top of it.

Counterpoint thinks display and some miscellaneous parts might actually get cheaper, offsetting the increases a bit. Camera costs are ticking up slightly with new tech, but nothing dramatic. Still, even with those offsets, Apple's margins are taking a hit unless they pass costs along.

And they will. Apple hasn't meaningfully raised Pro Max pricing since the iPhone 14 Pro Max jumped to $1,099 base, but the company has gotten creative about holding the line while extracting more revenue—killing the cheaper storage tiers, widening the gap between Pro and non-Pro, pushing people toward higher configs. A $300 component increase won't show up as a $300 price hike, but don't be shocked if the starting storage stays at 256GB and suddenly $1,299 doesn't sound so unreasonable for 512GB.

The real question is whether buyers will notice or care. iPhone ASPs keep climbing, and people keep buying. Apple's bet is that by the time the 18 ships, nobody will remember what the 17 cost anyway.
