[MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/17/apple-music-price-increase/) reported today that Apple Music is now $11.99 for individuals, up from $10.99. The Family plan jumped $3 to $19.99, and even students aren't spared—they're paying $6.99 now instead of $5.99. Apple's explanation: "rising licensing costs." That's the same line they used in October 2022, the last time they raised prices.

Here's the thing—licensing costs are real, and they're rising across the industry. Spotify, YouTube Music, and everyone else are dealing with the same pressure from labels. But Apple keeps positioning itself as the premium option, the service that respects artists and pays them fairly, and now it's charging a premium while Spotify's individual plan sits at $10.99. At some point, "we pay artists more" stops being a differentiating feature and starts feeling like a tax on loyalty.

The Family plan increase is especially brutal. Three dollars might not sound like much, but it's an 18% hike. For a household already juggling iCloud storage, Apple TV+, and maybe Fitness+, that's another nudge toward reconsidering the whole bundle. Apple One pricing went up too, though the company didn't break out exactly which tiers were affected. The student increase is almost comical—going from $5.99 to $6.99 is a 17% jump for the demographic least able to absorb it.

What Apple isn't saying is that Services revenue growth has been slowing. They need these price increases to keep the margin story intact, especially as hardware sales flatten. Music is an easy lever to pull because switching services is a hassle, playlists don't migrate cleanly, and most people won't cancel over a dollar. But the value gap between Apple Music and competitors is narrowing. Spatial Audio and lossless are nice, but they're not worth 20% more if you're just trying to stream pop songs on AirPods.

Apple last increased prices less than four years ago, and now they're back at it. If this becomes an annual ritual, the "rising costs" excuse is going to wear thin fast.
