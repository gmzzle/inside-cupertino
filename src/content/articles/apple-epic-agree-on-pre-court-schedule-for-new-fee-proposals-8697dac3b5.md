The Supreme Court declined Apple's request to pause the lower court's mandate, which means Apple and Epic are back at the negotiating table whether Apple likes it or not. [AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/05/16/apple-epic-agree-on-pre-court-schedule-for-new-fee-proposals?utm_source=rss) reported that the two companies filed a joint schedule with the District Court on May 15, laying out when they'll duke it out over Apple's commission rate for outbound payment links.

This is the gift that keeps on giving, legally speaking. The core issue remains what Apple can charge developers who want to link users to external payment systems instead of using Apple's in-app purchase setup. Apple's been charging 27% on those external transactions under its current rules, arguing it still deserves a cut for providing the platform. Epic thinks that's nonsense. The judge will ultimately decide what's reasonable, but first the two sides have to go through the motions of negotiating in good faith.

What's funny is how procedural this has become. Joint filings. Scheduled meetings. It's like watching two people who hate each other forced into couple's therapy by a judge. Apple lost the big fight when the Supreme Court refused to intervene, so now it's stuck in the slow grind of compliance. The company's strategy at this point seems to be dragging things out and maintaining as high a rate as possible for as long as possible.

The real question is whether Apple will actually negotiate in good faith or just go through the motions until a judge imposes a number. Given how hard Apple fought to avoid this moment entirely, betting on genuine compromise seems optimistic. More likely: both sides present their cases, the gap between them is enormous, and the District Court judge picks a percentage somewhere in the middle that makes nobody happy. That's how these things tend to end.
