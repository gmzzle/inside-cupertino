[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/18/eu-style-app-store-rules-reach-brazil-with-new-app-stores-payments?utm_source=rss) reported that Apple struck a deal with Brazil's antitrust regulator to bring alternative app marketplaces and payment systems to iOS 26.5. Same playbook as the EU's Digital Markets Act changes, but with a twist: Apple did this voluntarily to settle with CADE, Brazil's competition authority, rather than wait for a law to force the issue.

The timing here is telling. Apple fought the EU changes tooth and nail, only relenting when faced with massive fines. In Brazil, they're cutting a deal before things get ugly. That suggests Apple's legal team has done the math on global antitrust pressure and decided proactive settlements beat reactive compliance. Brazil is Latin America's largest economy and has been investigating Apple since 2022, so getting ahead of a formal ruling makes sense.

The mechanics mirror the EU setup almost exactly. Third-party marketplace operators need Apple's blessing and ongoing approval. Apps still go through Apple's notarization process, which means automated malware checks plus human review. Developers can use alternative payment processors, though Apple's cut of those transactions remains unclear from the announcement. The company has been creative about preserving revenue in the EU through Core Technology Fees and reduced-but-still-substantial commissions, and there's no reason to think Brazil will be different.

What's interesting is who comes next. Apple now has a template for settling with regulators without admitting the App Store model is fundamentally anticompetitive. Japan got alternate payment options in 2022. South Korea passed a law requiring them in 2021. The UK's Competition and Markets Authority is circling. If Apple can use Brazil as proof they'll negotiate in good faith, they might avoid a patchwork of incompatible regulations across dozens of markets.

The real test is whether any meaningful third-party marketplaces actually launch in Brazil, or if Apple's authorization requirements prove restrictive enough that the whole thing becomes theoretical.
