[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/21/as-garmin-cirqa-launches-should-apple-make-a-fitness-band-or-ring-poll/) is asking whether Apple should make a fitness band or ring, prompted by Garmin's new Cirqa Smart Band. The real question is why Apple would bother with a band at all.

Garmin's launching a screenless tracker makes sense for Garmin. They've always sold on battery life and discrete tracking, and a band fits that brand. But Apple already tried the minimal-wearable-tracker play. It was called the Nike+ FuelBand partnership, and before that, the original iPod shuffle clipped to your shirt. Apple's moved past accessories that do one thing poorly. If they're going to make something without a screen, it needs to be so unobtrusive you forget it's there. That's a ring, not another thing on your wrist competing with the Watch.

The Apple Watch owns the wrist. It's the company's second-biggest product line by revenue. Cannibalizing it with a cheaper band would be monumentally stupid, and Apple knows it. A ring, though, is a different category. It's for people who want health data but don't want notifications, apps, or a glowing rectangle. It's for people who already wear a mechanical watch. It's for people who find the Watch too much. That's a real market, and it doesn't step on the Watch's toes because the use cases barely overlap.

The tech is clearly there. Samsung has the Galaxy Ring. Oura's been iterating for years and just raised another round. Apple's filed ring patents going back to 2015. The real barrier isn't hardware—it's whether Apple thinks the market is big enough to justify the operational complexity of another product line with its own sizing, its own support matrix, its own SKU sprawl. Rings need to fit properly. That means more inventory, more returns, more hassle.

But if the question is band versus ring, it's not even close. A band is redundant. A ring is actually new.
