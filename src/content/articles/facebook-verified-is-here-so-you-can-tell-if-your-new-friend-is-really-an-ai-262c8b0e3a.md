Meta just rolled out Facebook Verified, a free badge that [AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/07/24/facebook-verified-is-here-so-you-can-tell-if-your-new-friend-is-really-an-ai?utm_source=rss) reports will show up on profiles to confirm you're chatting with an actual human and not a bot. The catch? It only verifies you're real, not that you're who you claim to be. So your new friend John Smith might genuinely be a person, but whether he's actually named John Smith or is a 47-year-old scammer in Belarus remains Facebook's eternal mystery.

This is separate from Meta Verified, the $12-a-month program Meta launched last year that does provide identity verification along with account protection and customer support. That one's still around. This new free version is Meta's answer to the AI spam problem currently eating Facebook alive—except it's solving it by creating yet another tier of trust signals that most users won't understand. You'll need to be 18-plus and in good standing with community standards to get the badge, which will show up in Marketplace, Dating, and Groups first before hitting the main feed.

The timing here matters. Facebook has become borderline unusable in many communities thanks to AI-generated spam accounts flooding Marketplace and Groups. Meta's own generative AI tools have made it trivially easy to create convincing fake profiles at scale. Now they're asking users to opt into a verification system to prove they're human—a problem that barely existed before Meta decided engagement metrics mattered more than platform integrity. It's like an arsonist selling fire extinguishers.

The real question is whether anyone will bother. A free badge that says "I'm definitely a person" but doesn't confirm identity has limited value when the platform is already overrun. And Meta's track record on verification isn't exactly confidence-inspiring—remember when they briefly let anyone buy a blue check for $12 and impersonators immediately ran wild? This feels like another half-measure designed to look like they're addressing the problem without actually fixing the underlying incentive structure that created it.
