[MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/21/ranked-best-features-rumored-iphone-18/) put together a ranked list of iPhone 18 rumors, sorting the credible from the wishful. Most of it tracks with what the supply chain has been murmuring for months: new Pro colors including Dark Cherry, more RAM across the lineup, and an iPhone Air 2 that finally gets a second camera after the first-gen model shipped with just one lens in 2026.

The variable aperture lens is the actual story here. If Apple ships it—and the sourcing looks solid—it would be the first meaningful camera hardware change since they added the 5x telephoto to the 15 Pro Max. Computational photography has been carrying iPhone cameras for years now, and it's great until it isn't. Variable aperture gives you mechanical control over depth of field and light intake, which means less reliance on software guessing what you want. Portrait mode could finally stop blurring earrings. Low-light shots could have actual creative latitude instead of just aggressive noise reduction and Smart HDR doing its best.

This also matters because the gap between Pro and non-Pro iPhones has been narrowing everywhere except the camera. The A-series chips are close enough that most people wouldn't notice. ProMotion is nice but not a dealbreaker. The camera system is the last pillar holding up the Pro's price premium, and variable aperture would be the kind of pro-focused feature that actually justifies the name instead of just meaning "costs more."

The split launch strategy—Pro models in September 2026, standard models in spring 2027—is still weird, and it's unclear if Apple can train customers to wait or if they're just going to cannibalize their own sales. The iPhone Air 2 getting a second camera makes sense, though. The original Air was a statement piece about thinness, but it was also kneecapped by that single-lens compromise. Adding an ultra-wide makes it a real option instead of a curiosity.

Variable aperture won't save a bad photo, but it might finally give iPhone photographers the one thing computational imaging can't fake: actual manual control that works in the moment.
