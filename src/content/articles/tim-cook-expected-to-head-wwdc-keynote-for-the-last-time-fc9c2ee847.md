[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/07/tim-cook-expected-to-head-wwdc-2026-keynote-for-the-last-time?utm_source=rss) is reporting that Tim Cook will probably kick off WWDC 2026 in June before handing most of the stage time to Craig Federighi, which sounds momentous until you realize that's exactly what happens at WWDC every year. Cook does the opening remarks, maybe a Services update, then Federighi owns the next 90 minutes talking about iOS and macOS while Cook watches from backstage.

The actual milestone here is that it'll be Cook's last keynote as CEO before John Ternus takes over in September. But WWDC was never Cook's show the way iPhone events are. He's the warm-up act for the software demos. The executives who live and breathe developer tools—Federighi, plus whoever's running the latest platform push—those are the people developers came to see. Cook just sets the tone and reminds everyone that Apple sold a lot of iPhones last quarter.

What makes this transition awkward is the timing. Ternus officially becomes CEO in September, which means his debut as the face of Apple will be the iPhone 18 event, not a developer conference. That's the right call. iPhone launches are where Apple flexes its consumer muscle and Ternus needs to own that moment. WWDC is a different beast—more technical, more inside baseball. Letting Cook handle one last developer crowd while Ternus preps for the main stage in fall makes sense.

The speculation that Cook is already letting Ternus drive future product planning also tracks. You don't announce a CEO transition six months early unless the new guy is already making the calls. Cook's victory lap through June is ceremonial. He'll shake hands, do the keynote, maybe get a standing ovation. Then he's gone and Ternus has to prove he can sell a phone.

So yes, this will technically be Cook's last WWDC keynote, but it'll look and feel like every other one since 2012: Cook talks, Federighi demos, developers clap.
