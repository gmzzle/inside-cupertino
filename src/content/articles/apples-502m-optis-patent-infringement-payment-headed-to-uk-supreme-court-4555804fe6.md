[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/29/apples-502m-payment-to-optis-over-patent-infringement-goes-to-uk-supreme-court?utm_source=rss) reports that Apple's still fighting a UK ruling that would hand Optis $502 million for allegedly infringing LTE patents — a case that started back in 2019 and has now reached the UK Supreme Court. The kicker? Apple already beat Optis in a US federal court this past February, avoiding a $300 million judgment on essentially the same patents.

Optis is a patent assertion entity — the polite term for a company that doesn't make products, just buys up old patents and sues people. They picked up a bunch of LTE-related IP and went after Apple in multiple jurisdictions at once, which is the standard playbook: file everywhere, hope something sticks. The US case fell apart. The UK case, apparently, did not.

The fact that two courts looked at similar claims and reached opposite conclusions isn't shocking — patent law is a mess, and UK and US standards diverge in important ways. But it does highlight how expensive and absurd these fights get. Apple's been litigating this for seven years now. Seven. That's longer than the iPhone 6 was relevant. Even if Apple wins at the UK Supreme Court, they've already spent a fortune on legal fees, and Optis has presumably been funded by someone willing to bankroll this war of attrition.

The UK ruling hanging over Apple's head isn't final, but $502 million is real money even for a company with Apple's cash pile. If the Supreme Court sides with Optis, Apple will likely appeal again or try to settle quietly. If Apple wins, Optis will probably find another angle. This is how patent trolls work: the process *is* the punishment.

Apple doesn't talk about these cases much, which makes sense — acknowledging them just validates the shakedown. But this one's been public long enough that the silence feels more like exhaustion than strategy.
