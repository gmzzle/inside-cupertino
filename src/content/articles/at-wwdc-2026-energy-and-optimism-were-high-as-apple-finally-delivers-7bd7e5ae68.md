[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/15/at-wwdc-2026-energy-and-optimism-were-high-as-apple-finally-delivers?utm_source=rss) caught the mood at WWDC 2026: developers are actually excited again. That's notable because the past two years of Apple Intelligence announcements have felt more like cautious beta tests than product launches.

The context matters here. WWDC 2024 introduced Apple Intelligence with a lot of "coming later" asterisks. WWDC 2025 added features but kept the training wheels on. By the time 2026 rolled around, the question wasn't whether Apple could do AI - it was whether they'd ever ship the full version they kept teasing. Apparently, they did.

What's interesting is how Apple played this. They could have rushed half-baked AI into iOS 18 and 19 to compete with Google and OpenAI's flashier demos. Instead, they took the heat for moving slowly and used that time to build something that actually works across their hardware. It's the classic Apple move: let everyone else ship first, then ship better. The risk is that by the time you show up, nobody cares anymore. Based on developer reactions, that didn't happen.

The energy shift is real because developers can finally build against stable AI APIs instead of chasing Apple's moving targets. That's what matters more than any keynote demo. When developers are optimistic, it means they see actual business opportunities in what Apple shipped - not just marketing promises.

Apple bet that users would tolerate a slower rollout if the final product was genuinely useful rather than impressively buggy. WWDC 2026 suggests that bet paid off, at least with the developer crowd that has to build on top of these features. Whether mainstream users feel the same way depends on whether Siri can finally handle a three-part request without losing its mind.
