[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/06/30/ios-27-makes-apples-shortcuts-app-exactly-what-ive-always-wanted/) is pretty excited about iOS 27's new "describe a shortcut" feature, and honestly, they're right to be. This is the feature that should've shipped when Apple started calling everything Apple Intelligence.

The pitch is simple: instead of assembling automation blocks like you're programming a dishwasher in 1987, you just describe what you want in plain English. "Turn off Wi-Fi and Bluetooth when I leave home." "Text my wife my ETA when I get in the car." The system builds the shortcut for you. It's the same natural language approach that made ChatGPT feel like magic to non-technical users, except now it's baked into the thing Apple has been insisting was too hard for normal people to use.

Shortcuts has always had this weird identity crisis. Power users love it because you can automate anything if you're willing to spend 45 minutes reading forum threads. Normal people open it once, see a wall of "Get Variable" and "If/Else" blocks, then never touch it again. Apple kept adding features for the Reddit crowd while the App Store filled up with "Shortcut tutorial" apps charging $4.99 to explain what should've been intuitive. The gap between Shortcuts' potential and its accessibility has been embarrassing for years.

What's telling is that this isn't really new AI capability. Google Assistant could build routines from descriptions back in 2019. Zapier's had natural language automation for a while. Apple's just finally admitting that visual programming interfaces don't actually make things easier for most humans. The smarter move is letting people describe their intent and translating that into the plumbing behind the scenes.

The real test will be whether the system can handle ambiguity and context the way a human would. If I say "remind me about this later," does it understand "this" means the webpage I'm looking at, and "later" probably means this afternoon, not in five minutes? Those interpretation layers are where natural language automation tends to fall apart. But if Apple nails it, Shortcuts might finally become the automation tool they've been pretending it was all along.
