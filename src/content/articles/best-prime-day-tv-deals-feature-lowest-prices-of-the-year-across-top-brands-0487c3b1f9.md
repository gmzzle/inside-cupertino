[MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/06/24/best-prime-day-tv-deals/) rounded up the TV deals hitting Prime Day, and yeah, the discounts are legitimate. Samsung, LG, Hisense—all the usual suspects are marked down 30-50% in some cases. If you're already paying for Prime and you've been waiting on a new TV, this is probably your moment.

But let's talk about that Prime membership requirement. Amazon wants $14.99/month or $139/year to unlock these deals, which effectively makes every "sale" $139 more expensive if you're not already subscribed. The 30-day trial dodge works once, but Amazon's counting on you forgetting to cancel or deciding the convenience is worth it. For a $2,299 Sony BRAVIA, fine—the membership cost is noise. For a $400 Hisense, you've just added 35% back to the "discount."

The Sony BRAVIA II at $2,299 is interesting mainly because Crutchfield is matching it, which tells you Sony set that price and Amazon's just playing along. If the sale is everywhere, it's not really a Prime exclusive—it's just June pricing. The real Prime Day advantage is the Lightning Deals on mid-tier sets that actually move units, not the flagship stuff that's on a predictable discount cycle anyway.

What's missing from most of these TV roundups is the part where Black Friday exists. Prime Day in June has become Amazon's dry run for November, and historically the November discounts have been slightly better on TVs because retailers are desperately clearing inventory for next year's models. If you can wait five months, you probably should. If you can't, these deals are solid enough—just don't fool yourself into thinking Prime Day invented the concept of a sale.
