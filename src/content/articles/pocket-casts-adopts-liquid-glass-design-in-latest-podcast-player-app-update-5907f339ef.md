[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/06/10/pocket-casts-adopts-liquid-glass-design-in-latest-podcast-player-app-update/) reported that Pocket Casts rolled out a Liquid Glass redesign this week, making it one of the first major third-party apps to fully embrace Apple's new visual language. It's a predictable move, but that doesn't make it wrong.

Liquid Glass is Apple's latest aesthetic push—the frosted, translucent panels and depth effects that showed up in iOS 20 and have been trickling through Apple's own apps ever since. When Apple sets a design direction this hard, third-party developers have two choices: adopt it and look native, or resist and look dated. Pocket Casts clearly decided looking native was the better play, especially for an app that lives in a crowded space where Apple Podcasts is the default.

The timing makes sense too. Pocket Casts has been through a couple ownership changes—Automattic bought it in 2018, spun it out in 2021, and it's been independent since. A visual refresh signals the app isn't stagnating, even if the core functionality hasn't changed much. Plus, Liquid Glass isn't just aesthetic—it's built around iOS 20's new rendering APIs, which means better performance and tighter system integration if you do it right.

What's interesting is how quickly the design language is spreading. Apple introduced Liquid Glass less than a year ago, and we're already seeing it in apps like Pocket Casts, Overcast, and even some smaller utilities. That's faster adoption than we saw with the iOS 7 flat redesign back in 2013, which took a full release cycle to really take hold. Part of that is Apple's SwiftUI updates making it easier to implement, but part of it is developers realizing users now expect the aesthetic consistency.

The real test for Pocket Casts isn't whether the frosted panels look nice—they do—it's whether the redesign makes the app easier to use or just prettier to screenshot. Early user reactions seem positive, but that honeymoon period always happens with redesigns. We'll know in a month whether this was a smart evolution or just keeping up with the Joneses in translucent UI.
