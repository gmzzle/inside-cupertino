[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/07/more-iphone-fold-dummy-shots-appear-in-folded-open-forms?utm_source=rss) posted new images from Sonny Dickson showing what's allegedly an iPhone Fold dummy unit in both folded and unfolded states. These follow an earlier leak from June 1 on Weibo. The design? It's a phone that folds in half. No wild hinge experiments, no triple-screen contraption, just the form factor Samsung and Motorola have been shipping for years.

Which is probably smart. Apple's never been first to a category, and when they finally show up, the play is always refinement over reinvention. The original iPhone wasn't the first smartphone. The Apple Watch wasn't the first smartwatch. They were just the first versions most people actually wanted to use. If this dummy is real, Apple's betting the same approach works for foldables: let everyone else work out the kinks, then ship something that doesn't feel like a science project.

The timing matters more than the design. Foldables have been around since 2019, but they've mostly stayed niche—expensive, fragile, weirdly thick when closed. Samsung's on its sixth or seventh generation of the Galaxy Fold depending on how you count, and the category still hasn't cracked the mainstream. Apple entering now suggests they think the tech is finally ready for normal people, or at least that waiting any longer means ceding a product tier to Samsung permanently.

What's missing from these shots is any hint of what makes this worth buying over a regular iPhone Pro. A bigger screen when unfolded is the obvious answer, but Apple's going to need more than that. The iPad mini exists. If this is just "iPhone screen, but sometimes larger," that's a tough sell at what'll probably be a $1,800 starting price. The software story—what iOS does on a folding display that it can't do on a slab—is the part these dummy units can't show.

Apple's going to announce this thing eventually, probably later this year if the dummy units are already circulating, and the real question isn't what it looks like but whether anyone outside the usual early adopters actually wants it.
