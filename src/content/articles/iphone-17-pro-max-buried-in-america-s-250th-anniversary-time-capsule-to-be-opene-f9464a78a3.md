[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/04/iphone-17-pro-max-included-in-america-250-time-capsule/) reports that someone decided an iPhone 17 Pro Max in Cosmic Orange should represent our technological moment for the next 250 years. It's now buried in a time capsule set to open in 2276 for America's 500th anniversary. The capsule includes items from every state, and apparently someone thought the best pick from the tech world was a phone that'll be obsolete before the dirt settles.

The irony is thick. By 2276, if anyone even remembers what an "iPhone" was, they won't be able to do anything with this one. The battery will be long dead - lithium-ion cells degrade even when not in use. iOS 21 (or whatever ships with the 17 Pro Max) won't connect to any network that exists 250 years from now. Apple's activation servers? Gone. The Lightning or USB-C port? A historical footnote. They're essentially burying an expensive paperweight that happens to have a nice orange finish.

What makes this particularly odd is that Apple moves faster than almost any other consumer company at making its own products obsolete. The iPhone 17 Pro Max will lose software support sometime around 2032, maybe 2033 if they're generous. Six years of updates, then it's done. Meanwhile, we're asking it to represent our era for two and a half centuries. The people who open this capsule will need a museum-grade charging cable and a working knowledge of extinct file systems just to see a boot logo.

The real artifact here isn't the phone - it's the optimism. We're still burial-ceremony excited about smartphones in 2026, still treating them like cultural touchstones worth preserving. Maybe that's the point. In 250 years, the iPhone itself will be incomprehensible, but the fact that we thought it mattered enough to bury? That tells its own story about what we valued. Even if the story is: we really loved rectangles that couldn't last a decade, let alone three centuries.
