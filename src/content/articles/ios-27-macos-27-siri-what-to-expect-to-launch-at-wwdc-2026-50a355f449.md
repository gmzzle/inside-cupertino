[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/05/ios-27-macos-27-siri-what-to-expect-to-launch-at-wwdc-2026?utm_source=rss) has the WWDC preview up, and the bones are what you'd expect: iOS 27, macOS 27, all the 27s. Developer betas drop right after the keynote on June 8. The beta split continues—version 26 gets bug fixes while 27 gets the new toys.

The real question isn't what version number Apple slaps on things this year. It's whether Siri finally stops being the punchline. Apple's been pitching AI as a multi-year project, but two years in, the assistant still can't reliably set two timers or understand follow-up questions that a $50 smart speaker handled in 2019. If WWDC 2026 is another round of "Siri can now do [thing it should've done in 2015]," that's a problem.

The broader AI story matters too. Apple Intelligence launched with a lot of infrastructure but not a lot of payoff for regular users. Writing tools are fine. Notification summaries are occasionally useful. Genmoji are Genmoji. But the stuff that would actually justify the "Intelligence" branding—proactive suggestions that don't feel like they're guessing, cross-app workflows that work, context that persists—has been slow to arrive. WWDC is the place to show that the foundation is actually supporting something.

Meanwhile, macOS 27 and iOS 27 will get their usual parade of incremental fixes and features that should've been there already. That's fine. That's what these updates do. But after years of Apple pitching itself as the privacy-focused AI company, the one that does things on-device and doesn't harvest your data, it needs to prove that approach actually delivers something better than the competition. Not just different. Better.

June 8. We'll see if they brought receipts or just another slide deck about how hard on-device processing is.
