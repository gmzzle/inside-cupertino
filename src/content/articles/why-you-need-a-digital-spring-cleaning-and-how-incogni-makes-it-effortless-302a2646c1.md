So [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/03/why-you-need-digital-spring-cleaning-how-incogni-makes-effortless-sponsored/) published what looks like a spring cleaning privacy explainer, complete with earnest warnings about data brokers harvesting your information. Except it's a sponsored post for Incogni, a service that scrubs your data from broker databases. The "(Sponsored)" tag is right there in the headline, but the whole thing is written like editorial.

This isn't new—tech blogs have been running native ads for years. But there's something particularly awkward about a publication that covers Apple doing a sponsored post about privacy. Apple's entire brand positioning for the last five years has been "privacy matters, and we're the ones who protect it." Privacy features are keynote tentpoles. They run Super Bowl ads about it. When a site covering that company turns around and monetizes privacy anxiety with a disguised ad, it undercuts the credibility needed to actually cover Apple's privacy moves critically.

The substance isn't wrong. Data brokers do hoover up your information. Services like Incogni, DeleteMe, and others will send removal requests on your behalf. It's tedious work most people won't do themselves. But that case can be made in an actual review or a clearly labeled product recommendation post. Framing it as "why you need" editorial—complete with the "we often overlook" concern-trolling—is just an ad with extra steps.

The bigger issue is what this does to reader trust. If someone skims past the "Sponsored" label and absorbs this as journalism, what happens when 9to5Mac publishes actual reporting on, say, Apple's data practices or a security vulnerability? The lines get blurry. Publications that cover companies obsessed with brand control should probably hold themselves to a similar standard when it comes to the wall between editorial and advertising.
