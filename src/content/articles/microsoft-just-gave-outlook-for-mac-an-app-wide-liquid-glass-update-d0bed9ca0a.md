Microsoft rolled out an "app-wide" Liquid Glass update to Outlook for Mac today, which [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/06/16/microsoft-just-gave-outlook-for-mac-an-app-wide-liquid-glass-update/) says should make it "look more at home" on the platform. Translation: Microsoft is finally catching up to the visual standards Apple set with macOS Big Sur back in 2020.

Liquid Glass is Microsoft's design language that leans on translucency, depth, and materials that feel less like Windows 95 and more like, well, actual operating systems in 2026. The company's been gradually applying it across Office apps, but Outlook has lagged. This update presumably brings rounded corners, softer shadows, and sidebars that don't look like they were imported from a different decade. It's the kind of polish that Mac users notice immediately when it's missing.

The timing matters because Outlook's had a rough couple of years on macOS. Microsoft deprecated the old, beloved Outlook 2019 and pushed everyone toward the new Outlook, which shipped half-baked and missing features people actually used. Microsoft 365 subscribers complained loudly. Some went back to Mail. Others stuck with the old app until Microsoft pulled the plug. This visual refresh won't fix missing Exchange rules or calendar bugs, but at least the app won't look quite as out of place next to Safari and Notes.

Microsoft's also been pushing hard to make its apps feel more native on Apple platforms lately—likely because it knows that if Outlook feels janky, people will switch. Apple's own Mail app isn't great, but it's *there*, and it doesn't feel like a Windows port. The real test is whether Microsoft commits to keeping Outlook on macOS feeling like a Mac app, or whether this is a one-time facelift before the next round of feature neglect.

For now, Outlook for Mac finally looks like it belongs. Whether it acts like it belongs is still the question.
