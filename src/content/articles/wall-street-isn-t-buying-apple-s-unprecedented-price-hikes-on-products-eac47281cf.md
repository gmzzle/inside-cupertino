Apple followed through on last week's warning and bumped prices across multiple product lines today. The official line, as [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/06/25/wall-street-isnt-buying-apples-unprecedented-price-hikes-on-products/) reported, is rising RAM costs. Wall Street's reaction? A collective shrug that pushed the stock down 3.2% in afternoon trading.

Here's the thing: RAM prices *have* climbed over the past six months, roughly 18% according to DRAMeXchange's spot pricing. But Apple doesn't buy RAM on the spot market like some startup scrambling for inventory. They lock in contracts quarters in advance with Samsung and SK Hynix, which means today's street prices shouldn't be hitting their cost structure until Q4 at the earliest. The timing here looks more opportunistic than reactive.

This also isn't how Apple usually operates. The company spent two decades training customers to expect the same price year after year, then surprise them with more capability for that same dollar. The iPhone held at $999 for the Pro model from 2017 through 2023. The MacBook Air sat at $999 from 2018 until the M2 bump. Even when component costs spiked during the pandemic, Apple ate the margin hit rather than move sticker prices. Telegraphing a price increase a week in advance and then blaming commodity costs? That's new, and it doesn't match the confidence of a company sitting on $162 billion in cash.

The analyst notes from Bernstein and Morgan Stanley both flagged the same concern: if Apple needs to pass through a mid-teens percentage cost increase this aggressively, either their margin targets have gotten more rigid or Services growth isn't covering hardware pressure the way they projected. Neither read is great. Apple's historically high gross margins—currently 46.8%—have always been the reward for premium positioning and operational discipline. Publicly citing input costs makes them look like everyone else.

Investors are right to be skeptical. This feels less like a necessary adjustment and more like testing how much pricing power Apple still has after a slow hardware cycle.
