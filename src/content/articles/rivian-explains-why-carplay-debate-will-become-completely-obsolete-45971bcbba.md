Rivian's software chief Wassym Bensaid told The Verge's Decoder podcast that his company still won't offer CarPlay because it "takes over every single pixel in the car," [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/05/28/rivian-software-chief-on-carplay/) reported. Fair enough. But then he pivoted to his real argument: AI agents will eventually make the whole CarPlay debate "completely obsolete."

This is the new playbook for automakers who don't want to admit they're protecting their walled garden. GM said something similar when they killed CarPlay support last year, though they at least had the decency to frame it around future software subscription revenue. Rivian's version is slicker - wrap it in AI futurism, talk about "agentic integration" and "wholesome user experiences," and hope people forget that CarPlay already works perfectly well today.

The problem with Bensaid's argument is the timeline. He admits this AI agent alternative will "take time to materialize." How much time? Three years? Five? Meanwhile, Rivian buyers are stuck with the company's native apps, which - let's be honest - will never match the ecosystem most iPhone users already live in. Apple Maps gets better every year. Apple Music, Podcasts, Audiobooks, Messages - they're all tightly integrated with the rest of your Apple devices. Rivian's system might be pretty, but it's an island.

The "every single pixel" complaint is revealing too. Rivian wants those pixels for their own UI, their own branding, their own future revenue streams. CarPlay doesn't have to take over everything - plenty of automakers have implemented it alongside native controls for climate, seats, and vehicle settings. Tesla proves you can have a cohesive software experience without CarPlay, but Tesla also has the scale and software chops that Rivian doesn't. Rivian delivered 57,000 vehicles last year. Apple sold 234 million iPhones. Which ecosystem do you think most Rivian buyers already trust?

The AI agent pitch might eventually pan out. But banking on vaporware to justify excluding the most popular smartphone integration platform today is a bet against your own customers.
