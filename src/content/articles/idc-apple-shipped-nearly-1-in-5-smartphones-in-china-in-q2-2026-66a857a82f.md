Apple shipped nearly one in five smartphones in China during Q2 2026, according to [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/14/idc-apple-shipped-nearly-1-in-5-smartphones-in-china-in-q2-2026/), citing new IDC data. That 19% share sounds solid until you realize Huawei also posted growth—and in a market where every other player contracted, that makes this a two-horse race Apple isn't clearly winning.

The context here matters. Huawei spent years getting hammered by U.S. sanctions, lost access to Google services and cutting-edge chips, and still clawed back to relevance with the Mate 60 series and its homegrown 5G chips. Chinese consumers responded. Not because Huawei's phones are better than the iPhone—they're not, and anyone honest will admit that—but because buying Huawei became a statement. Apple's growth in the same quarter shows the iPhone still has pull with Chinese buyers who want the best product, but it's increasingly swimming upstream against nationalist sentiment and aggressive local pricing.

Apple's problem isn't the iPhone 15 or even the iPhone 16. It's that China's economic slowdown is making consumers more price-conscious, and Huawei's flagships undercut Apple by hundreds of dollars while still feeling premium. Apple can't drop prices without torching its brand positioning everywhere else. It can't out-patriot a Chinese company. And it can't ignore a market that was once a quarter of its revenue.

The silver lining is that Apple grew at all. Most Android makers in China are in freefall—Xiaomi, Oppo, Vivo, all shrinking. Apple's holding share because it's not competing on price; it's competing on ecosystem lock-in and reliability. That's a defensible position, but it's also a ceiling. You can't grow share significantly when your cheapest phone costs more than most people want to spend.

Apple's China strategy now is basically damage control: don't lose the premium buyers, hope Huawei stumbles, and wait for the economy to recover. That's not a plan. That's a hope.
