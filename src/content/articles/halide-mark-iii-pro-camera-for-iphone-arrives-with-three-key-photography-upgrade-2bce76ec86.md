[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/27/halide-mark-iii-pro-camera-for-iphone-arrives-with-three-key-photography-upgrades/) reported that Halide Mark III is out today, roughly eighteen months after the team started teasing it. The delay makes sense when you see what shipped: manual focus peaking, a redesigned RAW processor, and real-time histogram overlays. These aren't features you slap together in a sprint.

What's notable is how much daylight still exists between Apple's first-party Camera app and what a third-party team can build on the same hardware. Apple spent years adding ProRAW and Photographic Styles, but the core shooting experience remains aggressively simplified. No zebras, no waveforms, no focus peaking unless you're shooting ProRes video. Halide fills that gap for the subset of iPhone owners who actually want manual control, and Mark III widens the feature gap further.

The timing is also interesting. Apple silicon in the iPhone 16 series finally gave third-party camera apps access to the same computational photography pipelines Apple uses, which means Halide can now process images with the same Deep Fusion and Smart HDR smarts baked into iOS. Before that, shooting RAW in Halide meant bypassing Apple's best image processing entirely. Mark III arrives at the exact moment when a third-party app can be both more powerful *and* visually competitive with Apple's output. That's new.

Halide's business model—$50/year or $200 lifetime—also highlights the constraint Apple puts on pro features. The Camera app will never charge for focus peaking because Apple doesn't do feature paywalls in stock apps, which means those features just won't ship at all if they're deemed too niche. A small independent team can serve a smaller audience and make the unit economics work. Apple can't, or won't.

For most people, the default Camera app is still more than enough. But if you've ever wished the iPhone let you nail manual focus or see real-time exposure data before you hit the shutter, Halide Mark III just became the obvious answer Apple refuses to build.
