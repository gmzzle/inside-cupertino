A brand-new iPhone 17 Pro Max in Cosmic Orange is now buried under Independence National Historical Park in Philadelphia, sealed until 2276. [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/06/iphone-17-pro-max-sealed-in-time-capsule/) reported that America250, the group running the country's semiquincentennial celebrations, picked the phone as the representative of American innovation for 2026. It won't see daylight again for 250 years.

The choice makes sense on paper - iPhones are ubiquitous, they're made by an American company, and they're genuinely impressive pieces of engineering. But there's something darkly funny about sealing away a $1,299 device that'll be technologically obsolete in three years, let alone 250. The people opening this in 2276 will probably look at an iPhone 17 the way we look at a rotary phone. Assuming they can even figure out how to turn it on without a compatible charger, or that the battery hasn't swollen into a small explosive after two and a half centuries.

The capsule supposedly includes "digital artifacts" stored in the Notes app - which is a choice. Notes will not be compatible with whatever operating system exists in 2276. The files might survive if the NAND flash storage doesn't degrade, but good luck finding something that can read iOS 20 or parse a .sqlite database from the 2020s. Digital preservation is brutally hard over decades, let alone centuries. We still have newspapers from 1776 that are readable. This phone will be a sealed brick.

What's actually going in that capsule is less a functional artifact and more a cultural statement about what we valued in 2026. The iPhone as totem, not tool. It says we thought handheld computers were important enough to represent us, which they are. It also says we apparently thought Apple's industrial design and marketing had staying power as symbols of American achievement. That might look prescient in 2276, or it might look like we buried a Betamax player. Either way, someone's getting a hell of an unboxing video.
