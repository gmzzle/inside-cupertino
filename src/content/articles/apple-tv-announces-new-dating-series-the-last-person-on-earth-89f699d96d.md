[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/23/apple-tv-announces-its-first-dating-series-the-last-person-on-earth/) reported that Apple TV+ is launching *The Last Person on Earth*, which they're carefully labeling a "documentary series" despite being, very obviously, a dating show. This is funny because everyone knows what it is, but Apple's brand team apparently needs the documentary fig leaf.

The naming gymnastics matter because Apple has positioned itself as the prestige streamer—the one that doesn't do cheap reality filler. They do Emmys. They do Oprah specials. They absolutely do not do *The Bachelor*. Except now they do, they're just calling it something else. It's the same instinct that made them initially resist live sports until the money got too good to ignore.

Reality dating shows print money and subscribers. Netflix's *Love Is Blind* costs a fraction of *The Crown* and delivers better retention numbers. Peacock has *Love Island*. Even Hulu has *The Kardashians*. Apple held out longer than anyone, but their subscriber growth has been flatlining for quarters now, and prestige dramas aren't cheap. *Masters of the Air* reportedly cost $250 million for ten episodes. You can make a lot of dating show seasons for that.

The "documentary" branding suggests they'll probably lean into emotional backstories and cinematic B-roll rather than hot tubs and champagne. Maybe some earnest narration about modern connection. Think *The Goop Lab* meets *Too Hot to Handle*. Whether that's actually more respectable or just more pretentious depends on the execution.

Apple TV+ still has the smallest content library of any major streamer, and they've been adding genres in careful increments: sports with MLS, live news with their morning show, now reality dating with a documentary asterisk. They're becoming a normal streaming service, just very slowly and with better lighting.
