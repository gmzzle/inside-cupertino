Apple's best work on Messages often happens when nobody's looking. [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/10/these-are-my-favorite-new-messages-features-in-ios-27-video/) walked through the new iOS 27 features, and the theme is refreshingly mundane: the app finally works the way you assumed it already did.

The standout is deeper Siri integration—you can apparently have Siri handle message context and replies without the usual ritual of confirming every word three times. That's huge. For years, dictating a text felt like teaching a toddler to tie shoes: technically possible, deeply annoying. If iOS 27 finally lets you fire off a message hands-free without wanting to hurl your phone into traffic, that's a win worth celebrating even if Apple didn't throw it a keynote slide.

The other additions are smaller but solve real irritations. Quick toggles for dictation mode and voice memo settings mean you're not diving into Settings every time you want to change how you communicate. These are the kinds of UI refinements that ship in .1 updates for most companies, but Apple's been weirdly stubborn about exposing common controls in Messages. Good to see them finally surface.

What's interesting is what Apple *didn't* do. No flashy visual redesign. No new sticker marketplace expansion. No blockchain nonsense. Just speed improvements and workflow fixes—the stuff that makes Messages feel less like it's fighting you. That restraint is either maturity or evidence that the iMessage team's bandwidth went entirely to RCS compatibility last cycle and they're still catching their breath.

Either way, Messages in iOS 27 sounds like the update nobody will notice until they realize they've stopped cursing at their phone.
