[MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/06/02/apple-design-award-winners-2026/) has the list of this year's Apple Design Award winners, released ahead of Monday's WWDC keynote. The awards span six categories, and as usual, it's a mix of delightful indie projects you've never heard of and choices that make you wonder if Apple's criteria shifted mid-meeting.

The standout oddity: the official NBA app won for Innovation. The NBA app. The one that's been around forever, showing you scores and streams. Maybe they rebuilt it from scratch this year—stranger things have happened—but calling the league's own app innovative feels like the committee needed a recognizable name and picked the safest brand in the room. Compare that to Grug, which won for Delight and Fun and sounds genuinely charming, or Moonlitt, a moon phase tracker that apparently nailed interaction design.

On the gaming side, Cyberpunk 2077: Ultimate Edition taking Visuals and Graphics makes sense on paper—it's a technically impressive port—but it's also a four-year-old game that launched as a disaster on other platforms. Apple loves celebrating big-name studios bringing AAA titles to its ecosystem, and this feels like that impulse winning out over spotlighting something built natively for Apple platforms from the ground up. Blue Prince and Consume Me, meanwhile, are indie titles most people won't recognize, which is actually the point of these awards: surfacing work that deserves attention.

The Inclusivity and Social Impact categories remain the hardest to evaluate from the outside. Guitar Wiz and Primary: News in Depth could be genuinely great at what they do, or they could be competent apps that checked boxes the judges valued. Without using them, it's hard to say, but at least Apple's trying to recognize more than just the prettiest interface.

What's clear is that the Design Awards continue to serve two audiences: developers who want validation for craft, and Apple's own PR machine, which wants to show the App Store as a place where both indie creativity and big-budget spectacle can thrive—even when one of the winners is just the NBA's app.
