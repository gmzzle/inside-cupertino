Apple and the Justice Department are in early settlement talks over the antitrust case filed back in 2024, [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/17/apple-doj-antitrust-settlement/) reports. The DOJ's lawsuit hit Apple on everything from super apps to third-party wallets to the way Messages kneecaps cross-platform messaging. Two years later, Apple's apparently offered some proposals and now both sides are negotiating.

Here's the thing: Apple already defanged this lawsuit. Since 2024, the company's rolled out a bunch of policy changes that directly address the DOJ's complaints. Third-party app stores on iOS in the EU. RCS support in Messages. Tweaks to how the App Store handles cloud gaming and wallet functionality. Whether those changes were purely compliance theater or actual policy evolution doesn't matter much—they still undercut the government's argument that Apple is incapable of reform without a court order.

The DOJ tried to paint Apple as this locked-down ecosystem deliberately suffocating competitors. But when the company can point to actual, implemented changes that line up with the lawsuit's demands, suddenly the case looks less like "monopolist refuses to budge" and more like "company adapts faster than litigation." Apple already tried to get the case dismissed last year and failed, but settlement talks suggest both sides see the writing on the wall. The government doesn't want to spend years litigating a case that's getting weaker by the quarter, and Apple doesn't want discovery or a trial that puts internal strategy docs in the public record.

No guarantee they'll actually reach a deal, and we don't know what Apple's offering. But the fact that talks are happening at all tells you Apple thinks it can negotiate its way out cheaper than fighting. That's not weakness—it's just math. The company's already made the changes. Might as well get credit for them in a settlement and move on.

Either way, this case was never going to be the one that breaks Apple's back. Too diffuse, too many moving targets, and the company's too good at strategic compliance. If a settlement lands, expect Apple to call it a win and the DOJ to claim victory for forcing reform. Everyone goes home happy except the lawyers.
