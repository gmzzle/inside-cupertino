Apple's apparently got multiple new iPads coming later this year, [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/04/30/new-ipads-will-launch-later-this-year-heres-what-rumors-say-is-coming/) reports, which is standard operating procedure at this point. The problem isn't that new iPads are coming—it's that the current lineup makes zero sense and nothing in the rumor mill suggests Apple's going to fix that.

The iPad product matrix right now spans eight models across four product lines, with chips ranging from the A14 to the M4, prices from $349 to $2,599, and feature sets that overlap in completely baffling ways. The base iPad still has a Lightning port while the Air has USB-C. The 11-inch iPad Pro costs $350 more than the 11-inch iPad Air despite looking nearly identical. The Mini exists in some quantum state between serious product and afterthought.

What Apple needs isn't more iPads. It needs fewer iPads with clearer use cases. Keep the Pro for pros who actually need ProMotion and the M-series horsepower. Make the Air the default iPad for everyone else. Refresh the Mini consistently or kill it. Deprecate the base iPad entirely—it's a USB-C dongle away from being the same device as the Air anyway, and the price gap is already narrow enough that most people should just spend the extra $250.

Instead, the rumor mill suggests we're getting iterative updates across multiple lines, which means the spring 2027 buyer is still going to stand in an Apple Store wondering why there are eight rectangles that all run iPadOS but cost wildly different amounts. That's not a product lineup. That's a product problem.

Apple used to be ruthless about killing products that didn't earn their place. The iPad lineup could use some of that old discipline.
