Masimo's legal offensive against Apple took another hit this week when the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia dismissed the company's complaint challenging the USITC's refusal to reinstate an Apple Watch import ban. [AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/04/25/massimos-apple-watch-ban-complaint-dismissed-by-us-district-court?utm_source=rss) reported the April 24 filing shows the dismissal came with prejudice, meaning Masimo cannot refile the same claim.

This marks the third significant setback for Masimo since the ITC's original December 2023 exclusion order against certain Apple Watch models. That order forced Apple to temporarily halt sales and remove pulse oximetry features from newer units. But the company redesigned around Masimo's patents within weeks, resuming full sales by mid-January 2024. When Masimo asked the ITC to reinstate the ban in early 2025, arguing Apple's workaround still infringed, the Commission declined. Masimo then sued the ITC itself in District Court—a rare and aggressive move that has now failed.

The pattern suggests Masimo overestimated the strength of its position. Apple generated roughly $42 billion from Wearables, Home, and Accessories in fiscal 2024, with Apple Watch representing the largest share. A sustained ban could have meaningfully dented that revenue, but only if Masimo's patents proved both valid and impossible to design around. Apple's rapid hardware revision demonstrated neither condition held. The ITC's reluctance to grant a second exclusion order likely reflects skepticism that Masimo's claims cover genuinely novel territory Apple cannot avoid.

Masimo, which makes hospital pulse oximeters, has positioned itself as a David fighting Goliath. But its litigation strategy increasingly looks like a bid to extract a licensing settlement rather than a principled defense of core IP. Apple has shown no interest in settling, instead investing in engineering workarounds and contesting every procedural step. With the District Court now closed as an avenue and the ITC unwilling to revisit its decision, Masimo's remaining options narrow to appealing on different grounds or pivoting to a damages-only claim in separate ongoing litigation.

The dismissal with prejudice closes the door on this particular complaint, but the underlying patent disputes continue in multiple venues. What's clear is that Masimo has yet to land a punch that changes Apple's product roadmap or market position in any lasting way.
