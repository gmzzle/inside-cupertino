Apple just made it easier to pretend iPads don't cost as much as they actually do. [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/07/15/apple-adds-36-month-carrier-financing-for-ipads/) reported that AT&T and Verizon are now offering 36-month financing for cellular iPads, tripling the 12-month Apple Card option that's been the standard forever.

The math is obvious: spread $1,299 for an iPad Pro over three years instead of one, and suddenly it's $36 a month instead of $108. Same total cost, way less monthly pain. It's the phone carrier playbook applied to tablets, which makes sense given how aggressively Apple has pushed LTE and 5G into every iPad tier. The catch? You need to activate a new line. No piggybacking on your existing plan, which means an extra $10-20 monthly for most people on top of the device payment.

This is Apple admitting iPad pricing has gotten out of hand without actually lowering prices. The M4 iPad Pro starts at $999 for the 11-inch with 256GB, and the 13-inch with cellular and 1TB will run you north of $2,000. The iPad mini is $499 base but $649 for cellular. These aren't impulse purchases anymore, and Apple Card's 12-month window doesn't stretch far enough when the average buyer is looking at $800-1,500 configurations.

The real winners here are the carriers, not customers. They get multi-year commitments on data lines that most people will barely use - how often are you streaming 4K on your iPad away from Wi-Fi? - and Apple gets to keep iPad ASPs high while moving units. It's the same financing sleight of hand that made $1,000+ iPhones feel normal. Make the monthly number small enough and people stop doing the total-cost math.

Apple pulled carrier financing for iPhones years ago to push people toward upgrade programs and Apple Card. Bringing it back for iPads suggests the tablet business needs help moving premium models more than it needs to own the financing relationship.
