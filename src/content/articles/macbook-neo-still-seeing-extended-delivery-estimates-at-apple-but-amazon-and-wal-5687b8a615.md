The MacBook Neo is selling faster than Apple can restock it. [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/04/27/macbook-neo-amazon-walmart/) reports 2-3 week delays across every Neo configuration on Apple.com, while Amazon and Walmart are shipping as soon as tomorrow. Amazon's even throwing in a $9 discount, which is basically nothing but still more than Apple's offering right now.

This is the first time in years Apple's had a laptop hit this hard out of the gate. The $549 starting price clearly found a nerve — it undercuts the Air without feeling like a compromise, and the education pricing at $499 makes it an obvious campus pick. Apple nailed the positioning, but they didn't anticipate the volume. Or maybe they did and still couldn't get enough units through the supply chain in time.

What's interesting is how quickly third-party retail filled the gap. Amazon and Walmart got their allocations and held stock while Apple's own pipeline went dry. That's not how it used to work — Apple historically kept the tightest control over launch inventory, and if you wanted it fast, you went to Apple. Now the fastest way to get a Neo is through a retailer Apple doesn't even fully control the messaging at. It's a pragmatic move, but it also means Apple's ceding some of the launch experience to whoever's running the Amazon listing that day.

The $9 discount is almost insultingly small, but it doesn't need to be bigger. People aren't price shopping the Neo — they just want it now. Amazon's real edge is speed, not savings. If you're a Prime member in a major metro, same-day shipping beats waiting three weeks for the privilege of buying directly from Apple. Walmart's playing the same game without the discount, banking on Walmart+ subscribers who'd rather consolidate shipping than hunt for deals.

Apple will restock eventually, but this launch showed the limits of their retail control when demand actually surprises them.
