[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/05/04/testing-common-myths-for-your-portable-mac-to-extend-your-battery-life?utm_source=rss) tested the usual battery life myths, and the results are about what you'd expect: most of the variables that matter were locked in when your Mac left the factory. Screen brightness matters. Closing Chrome tabs matters if you have 47 of them open. But the obsessive battery optimization ritual? Mostly theater.

Apple keeps adding battery health settings with each macOS release. The latest batch landed in macOS 15.4, joining optimized charging, low power mode, and the battery health menu that's been around since Big Sur. These features work, technically. Optimized charging does reduce long-term degradation by learning your charging patterns and holding at 80% until you need the full charge. Low power mode throttles background processes and dims the screen. The problem is that none of this changes the fundamental equation: lithium-ion batteries degrade based on charge cycles and heat, and your usage pattern is what it is.

The real advice is boring. Keep your Mac out of hot cars. Don't leave it plugged in at 100% for months straight if you can avoid it. Use it normally and replace the battery in three years when capacity drops below 80%. Apple's battery health tools are fine as passive safeguards, but they're not worth micromanaging. The people who obsess over keeping their battery at 40-80% charge are buying themselves maybe six extra months of capacity at the cost of constantly thinking about their battery. That's not a good trade.

The myth-testing genre exists because people want control over expensive hardware, and batteries feel like the one thing you can still influence. But a 2023 MacBook Air battery costs $159 to replace through Apple. If you're spending mental energy on charge level anxiety, you're valuing your time at about $4 an hour. Just use your Mac.
