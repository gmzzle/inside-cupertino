[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/01/apple-escalates-fight-with-india-antitrust-watchdog-over-access-to-global-financials/) reported that Apple is now accusing India's Competition Commission of overstepping its authority by demanding access to the company's global financials as part of an antitrust investigation. This isn't a routine objection. Apple is essentially arguing the CCI is acting beyond its jurisdiction—crossing from regulator into judge.

The fight matters because India is Apple's fastest-growing major market and the CCI has been increasingly aggressive about tech platforms. The commission opened an antitrust probe into Apple's App Store practices back in 2021, and this data demand appears connected to that case. Apple's position is that a national competition authority has no business rifling through worldwide revenue and cost structures when the alleged violations are India-specific. It's a jurisdictional argument, but it's also a practical one: give one regulator that kind of access and twenty more will want it.

Apple has played this game before. In the EU, it fought tooth and nail over data requests during the Digital Markets Act process. In the US, it's spent years limiting what the DOJ can subpoena in its own antitrust case. The company has a clear playbook: contest scope, contest jurisdiction, contest relevance. Slow everything down and force regulators to justify every ask in court. It works more often than it doesn't.

What's different here is that India's regulatory environment is less predictable than Brussels or Washington. The CCI has a history of aggressive penalties and loose procedural standards by Western benchmarks. Apple probably sees this as a line-drawing exercise. If the CCI gets everything it wants now, the precedent makes every future investigation in India that much harder to manage. Better to fight it out early and establish boundaries.

The irony is that Apple is simultaneously courting India as a manufacturing hub and growth market while lawyering up against its regulators at every turn. That's sustainable for now, but it's the kind of tension that doesn't age well.
