[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/16/indie-app-spotlight-cats-lock-is-a-fun-mac-app-that-solves-cat-keyboard-spam/) highlighted Cats Lock this week, and honestly, it's the kind of indie Mac utility that makes you wonder why Apple hasn't built this in already. The premise is simple: when your cat walks across your keyboard, the app detects the chaos and blocks the input. No more random Slack messages. No more accidentally nuking a paragraph you've been editing for twenty minutes.

The broader story here is that macOS still doesn't have great native tools for this kind of situational input management. Hot Corners were supposed to solve adjacent problems back in the Exposé days, but they require you to *remember* to use them. Cats Lock, presumably, just works in the background. That's the ideal Mac utility: invisible until you need it, then quietly heroic.

It's also a reminder that indie developers are still finding meaningful gaps in the Mac experience, even after 40 years of the platform. Apple's focused on Stage Manager and Continuity Camera and all these marquee features, but someone working on a laptop at home with a pet has a completely different set of friction points. Cats Lock isn't going to move the needle on Mac sales, but it'll make a few thousand people's lives measurably better, and that's the whole point of the indie app ecosystem.

The app joins a small but proud tradition of niche Mac utilities that solve one thing perfectly. Think Bartender for menu bar management, or Amphetamine for keeping your Mac awake. These aren't apps Apple would ever build, but they're the reason people still prefer the Mac for workflows that need customization. You can actually fix your problems instead of working around them.

Cats Lock is free to try, and if your cat has ever sent a string of "jjjjjjjjkkkkkk" to your boss, it's probably worth installing.
