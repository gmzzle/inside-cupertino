Apple launched digital driver's licenses in Wallet back in 2021, and [AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/25/11/18/how-to-use-digital-ids-in-apple-wallet-where-they-are-supported?utm_source=rss) just published a guide on how to use them. The problem is the guide basically amounts to: you probably can't.

Five states support digital IDs in Apple Wallet as of late 2024: Arizona, Colorado, Georgia, Hawaii, and Maryland. That's it. California has been "coming soon" since 2022. Florida announced support, then pulled back. The TSA accepts them at select airport security checkpoints, which is genuinely useful if you're flying out of Phoenix or Denver. But try showing your iPhone to a cop during a traffic stop or a bartender checking your age, and you'll get a blank stare followed by "I need to see your actual ID."

The friction isn't Apple's fault. This is a state-by-state regulatory slog involving DMVs, law enforcement training, retail compliance, and privacy frameworks that nobody agrees on. Apple built the tech - encrypted, biometrically protected, selective disclosure so you can prove you're over 21 without revealing your address. It's arguably more secure than a laminated card. But security doesn't matter if the bouncer at your local bar has never seen a digital ID and doesn't trust it.

The use case Apple keeps pushing is TSA PreCheck lanes, where it works smoothly. You tap your phone, Face ID confirms it's you, and you're through. No fumbling for your license. But airports are a controlled environment with standardized equipment. The real test is ubiquity - grocery stores, traffic stops, hotels, banks. We're nowhere close.

Until digital IDs are accepted everywhere physical IDs are legally required, you're still carrying your wallet. Which means the whole point - ditching the physical card - doesn't actually happen.
