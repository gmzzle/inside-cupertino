[MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/06/16/snap-specs-ar-glasses/) reported that Snap launched Specs today, a $2,195 pair of AR glasses with dual Snapdragon chips, full-color cameras, and a 51-degree field of view. They're actual see-through glasses you wear on your face, not a ski goggle strapped to your head.

This matters because Apple has spent years telegraphing AR glasses as the future while shipping exactly zero pairs of them. Vision Pro was supposed to be a stepping stone, but at $3,499 and 600 grams, it's not stepping anywhere except your living room couch. Snap's Specs weigh 136 grams max and come in two frame sizes with swappable prescription lenses. You can wear them outside without looking like you're cosplaying a cyberpunk extra.

The specs aren't consumer-ready either, to be clear. That price point screams developer kit, and the 51-degree field of view is narrow enough that AR content lives in a box in front of you rather than seamlessly overlaying the world. But Snap is shipping hardware, collecting real-world usage data, and iterating in public. Apple's AR roadmap remains a rumor mill of supply chain whispers and patent filings. The company that defined wearables with Apple Watch is now watching Snap, Meta, and even Xreal put actual AR glasses in people's hands.

Apple's calculus has always been to wait until the tech is good enough to feel like magic rather than compromise. That strategy gave us the iPhone instead of a Windows Mobile knockoff. But AR is different—the learning curve isn't just hardware, it's figuring out what people actually want to do while wearing computers on their faces. You can't do that in a lab. Snap's been running that experiment since 2016 with Spectacles, and now they're on a version with actual AR capabilities instead of just cameras.

Apple will eventually ship lightweight AR glasses that make Specs look like a beta test. But "eventually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
