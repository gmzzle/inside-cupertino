So here's a twist in the Apple-OpenAI lawsuit that would be funny if it weren't potentially derailing a major IP case: OpenAI says it *did* respond to Apple's trade secret theft allegations. Apple's lawyers apparently just sent their complaints to the wrong email address and never noticed the replies sitting in some other inbox.

[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/07/15/openai-blames-email-mixup-for-why-it-didnt-respond-to-apple-trade-theft-claims?utm_source=rss) reports that OpenAI has shared emails with NBC showing they responded to Apple's claims—directly contradicting Apple's assertion that OpenAI ghosted them entirely. This is the kind of procedural screwup that makes legal teams look incompetent and gives the other side ammunition to argue bad faith. Apple's lawyers claimed OpenAI's silence was effectively an admission. OpenAI's lawyers are now saying Apple wasn't even looking in the right place.

For context, Apple's lawsuit centers on allegations that OpenAI improperly used Apple's proprietary data to train its models. The lack of response was supposedly a major factor in Apple deciding to escalate to litigation—painting OpenAI as unwilling to even engage on the issue. That narrative collapses if it turns out Apple's own team couldn't keep track of their email threads. It's also the kind of detail that can poison a judge's perception of your case before you even get to the merits.

The obvious question: how does a company with Apple's resources and legal firepower manage to misroute correspondence in a case this significant? Corporate email systems are a mess, sure, but this isn't some startup. This is Apple. And if OpenAI's emails are legitimate—and NBC apparently saw them—then Apple's opening move in this lawsuit was based on a factual error that should have been caught in discovery, if not earlier.

OpenAI still has to defend against the underlying IP theft claims, but this gives them a procedural win and a PR talking point. Apple looks sloppy. Not a great start.
