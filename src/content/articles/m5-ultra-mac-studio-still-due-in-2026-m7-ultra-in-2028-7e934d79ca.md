[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/28/m5-ultra-mac-studio-still-due-in-2026-m7-ultra-in-2028?utm_source=rss) reports the Mac Studio is getting the M5 Ultra in 2026 and the M7 Ultra in 2028, which means Apple's now comfortable letting its most powerful desktop sit untouched for two full years at a stretch. The M4 generation got skipped entirely for the Ultra chip, and apparently that's the template going forward.

This makes a certain kind of sense from Apple's perspective. Ultra chips require two Max dies fused together, which is expensive and complex. The Mac Studio audience is small - pros who need more than a Mac mini but don't want the modularity tax of a Mac Pro. Updating every other generation keeps R&D focused on the chips that actually drive volume: the base M-series in MacBook Air, the Pro in MacBook Pro, the Max in the high-end laptops and iMac. The Ultra becomes a special occasion.

But it's a weird product to recommend now. If you bought an M2 Ultra Mac Studio in 2023, you're looking at a 2026 replacement at the earliest. That's a three-year gap, which is fine for a desktop. But if you're shopping today in mid-2025, do you buy an M2 Ultra that's already two years old, or wait another year for the M5 Ultra? The value proposition gets murky fast when the current model is that stale.

The M7 Ultra arriving in 2028 suggests Apple sees the Mac Studio as a platform that updates when the Ultra chip updates, full stop. No spec bumps, no interim refreshes, no design changes - AppleInsider notes the form factor is expected to stay the same. It's the anti-iPhone strategy: build something solid, let it ride, update when there's a meaningful performance leap. That works if you're targeting studios and labs with multi-year budgets, less so if you're a freelancer trying to time a purchase.

The Mac Studio is becoming Apple's desktop equivalent of the Mac Pro's old tower days - a machine for people who buy once, use hard, and don't think about upgrades until the wheels fall off.
