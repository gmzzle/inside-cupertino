Five years in, AirTags have done something unusual for Apple: they created a hardware ecosystem that Apple doesn't entirely control. [Six Colors](https://sixcolors.com/post/2026/04/find-my-diversifies-as-hardware-makers-go-for-baroque/) noted the range of Find My-compatible devices now on the market, and it's genuinely weird how broad it's gotten. Chipolo card trackers, Belkin key rings, even bike bells and luggage locks. Apple opened the Find My network to third parties in 2021, handed them the spec, and then largely got out of the way.

That's not how Apple usually operates. Typically, if third parties get access to an Apple feature, there's a review process, brand policing, and a lot of "courage" talks on stage. Here, though, Apple seems content to let the network grow sideways. The AirTag itself hasn't changed since launch — same CR2032 battery, same speaker everyone complains is too quiet, same precision finding that only works if you're within Bluetooth range and own a U1-equipped iPhone. Meanwhile, Chipolo and others are iterating, adding features like rechargeable batteries and louder alerts.

The result is that Find My has become something closer to a protocol than a product line. Apple still sells AirTags and only AirTags, but the broader ecosystem looks more like HomeKit or CarPlay: Apple provides the infrastructure, third parties fill in the gaps Apple won't. It's a rare moment of restraint. Apple could have locked this down, required MFi certification fees, or built ten different AirTag variants. Instead, they built the network, made it work shockingly well, and let others run with it.

The downside? Apple's own AirTag lineup is stale. No AirTag 2, no card-shaped variant, no rechargeable option. If you want those, you go third-party, which is fine — except it means Apple is ceding product design in a category it invented. Maybe that's the trade-off. Apple gets a massive, crowd-sourced tracking network that makes iPhones stickier. Everyone else gets to sell gear that only works if you're already in Apple's ecosystem. It's symbiotic, but it's also Apple admitting they don't need to make everything themselves anymore.
