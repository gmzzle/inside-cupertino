Apple is planning a hardware redesign for the iPhone's 20th anniversary in 2027, and according to [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/04/new-iphone-redesign-is-coming-next-year-inspired-by-liquid-glass-report/), it's being built to visually sync with iOS 26's Liquid Glass design language. The idea is that the physical device and the software interface will feel like parts of the same aesthetic whole, not just a screen bolted to a metal frame.

This is a familiar Apple move—hardware and software designed in tandem—but it's also riskier than it sounds. iOS redesigns come and go. Remember iOS 7's skeuomorphism purge in 2013? That reset lasted years, but Liquid Glass is still mostly a concept we've only seen in leaks and early betas. Locking in hardware around a software aesthetic means betting that users will love Liquid Glass enough to keep it around. If iOS 27 or 28 pivots again, you're stuck with a phone that looks like it was designed for an OS two versions ago.

The 20th anniversary framing is deliberate. Apple loves milestone moments—see the iPhone X for the 10th—but those redesigns have to justify themselves beyond symbolism. The X brought Face ID and an edge-to-edge display. What does a Liquid Glass-inspired iPhone bring? Translucent materials? Curved edges that mimic the UI's fluidity? If it's just a repackaging of existing tech with a new finish, that's a hard sell, especially if the price tag climbs again.

Apple hasn't officially confirmed any of this, and 2027 is far enough out that plans could shift. But if the company is serious about hardware that "blends seamlessly" with iOS, it's a sign they think Liquid Glass is more than a visual refresh—it's a platform they plan to build on for years. That's either visionary or a very expensive design risk.
