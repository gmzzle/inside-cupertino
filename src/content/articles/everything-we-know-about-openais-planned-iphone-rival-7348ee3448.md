OpenAI is apparently building a smartphone. [MacRumors](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/05/29/everything-we-know-about-openai-iphone-rival/) reports analyst Ming-Chi Kuo's supply chain checks point to an "AI agent phone" launching in the second half of 2026, running on a customized MediaTek Dimensity 9600 chip. The pitch is that you won't need apps anymore—just tell the phone what you want and it figures out how to do it.

This is a terrible plan. Not because AI agents are a bad idea. They're not. The problem is OpenAI seems to think the only way to deliver this experience is to build the entire stack themselves, which means competing with Apple in hardware. That's like deciding the best way to improve restaurant delivery is to manufacture your own cars. Hardware is brutally hard, brutally capital-intensive, and OpenAI has exactly zero experience doing it at consumer scale. Apple spent decades and hundreds of billions of dollars building the manufacturing, retail, and support infrastructure that makes the iPhone work. OpenAI's going to spin that up in 18 months with a MediaTek chip?

The whole premise is also flawed. Kuo's argument is that controlling the OS and hardware is "the only way" to deliver comprehensive AI agents. Except it's not. Apple already lets developers run sophisticated on-device models. Google's done the same with Android. If OpenAI's agent capabilities are genuinely better, they could ship them as apps or OS-level integrations right now. They don't need their own phone—they need better APIs and partnerships. This smells like empire building, not product strategy.

The MediaTek detail is telling, too. Apple uses custom silicon that's years ahead of anything else in mobile. OpenAI's outsourcing their chip to a vendor whose flagship parts trail the A-series by a generation or two. That's fine for a $300 Android phone, but for a premium AI device that's supposedly going to replace your iPhone? The performance ceiling matters.

This will either get quietly canceled before launch or ship to minimal adoption and die within two years. Building a successful smartphone in 2026 requires more than a clever interface idea—it requires an ecosystem, carrier relationships, supply chain mastery, and a reason for millions of people to switch. OpenAI has none of those things.
