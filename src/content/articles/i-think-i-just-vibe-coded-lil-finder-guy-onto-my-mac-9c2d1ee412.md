OpenAI pushed out a Codex update on Friday that includes, of all things, customizable desktop pets in the Tamagotchi vein. [9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/01/i-think-i-just-vibe-coded-lil-finder-guy-onto-my-mac/) reported someone already "vibe coded" a creature called Lil Finder Guy that now hovers over their Mac Dock, which feels like exactly the kind of chaotic Friday energy this feature was designed to unleash.

The interesting bit isn't that AI can generate a bouncing sprite—that's table stakes at this point. It's that OpenAI is positioning Codex as a tool for spontaneous, low-stakes creation. You're not building a production app here. You're making a little guy who lives on your desktop because the vibes were right. It's the coding equivalent of doodling in a notebook margin, except the doodle can follow your cursor around.

This sits oddly with Apple's recent push toward more buttoned-up, utilitarian AI features. While Cupertino has been carefully productizing intelligence into things like Smart Replies and photo search, OpenAI is letting people spin up desktop companions on a whim. It's a different philosophy: Apple wants AI that disappears into usefulness; OpenAI is fine with AI that's just... there. Hanging out. Being a little guy.

The real question is whether this sticks or becomes another feature people try once and forget. Desktop pets had a moment in the late 90s with things like eSheep and Bonzi Buddy, and they faded because novelty alone doesn't justify persistent screen real estate. If OpenAI's version survives, it'll be because people can make them do something genuinely useful—or at least funny enough to keep around.

For now, Lil Finder Guy is out there, vibing on someone's Dock, which is either the future of human-computer interaction or a very silly footnote in the AI hype cycle.
