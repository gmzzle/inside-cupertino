[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/29/whatsapps-new-username-system-keeps-phone-numbers-private-by-design?utm_source=rss) reported that WhatsApp opened username reservations on June 29, with a wider rollout coming later this year. The feature lets people connect without swapping phone numbers—useful for group chats, casual meetups, or anyone who doesn't want their digits floating around.

This is fine, but it's also incredibly late. Telegram added usernames in 2015. Signal has had them since 2024. WhatsApp, despite being the biggest messaging platform on the planet with over 2 billion users, is rolling this out in 2026. That's not innovation—it's playing catch-up with a feature that stopped being novel a decade ago.

The privacy angle makes sense, especially for group chats where your phone number gets broadcast to dozens of strangers. WhatsApp says it won't build a public directory or suggest accounts based on usernames, which is the right call. But the real question is why Meta waited this long. The company has spent years pitching end-to-end encryption as its privacy north star, yet it's been fine with phone numbers as the only way to connect until now.

The timing feels reactive. Signal's username rollout got attention. iMessage stays locked to Apple ID or phone number, which gives WhatsApp an opening to look more flexible. And Meta's been under pressure to show it takes privacy seriously after years of scandals. Adding usernames is low-hanging fruit—easy to build, easy to spin as a privacy win.

WhatsApp remains the dominant cross-platform messaging app, and most people don't care about usernames enough to switch. But this update is less about pulling ahead and more about not falling too far behind.
