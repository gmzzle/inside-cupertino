[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/05/01/whatsapp-working-to-bring-liquid-glass-to-the-in-chat-interface/) reports that WhatsApp is now working to bring Apple's Liquid Glass design language into individual chat screens, not just the main Chats list. This is the visual overhaul Apple introduced with iOS 18 — translucent panels, refined frosting effects, the whole spatial computing aesthetic trickling down to everyday apps.

What's interesting here isn't that WhatsApp is adopting iOS design conventions. Third-party apps have done that forever. It's that Meta is taking its time with this rollout, treating Liquid Glass like a major feature rather than just updating a few blur layers and calling it a day. The main Chats screen has been getting the treatment in stages, and now they're moving deeper into the app. That suggests Meta sees real value in matching Apple's visual language — probably because users do notice when an app feels off-brand for the platform.

The timing matters too. Apple spent years making iMessage feel like the default, the one that belongs. Part of that was features like Tapbacks and reactions, sure. But a lot of it was just visual cohesion. iMessage looks like it was built by the people who built iOS, because it was. WhatsApp, for all its dominance outside the US, has always felt a bit like a visitor on iOS. Liquid Glass won't fix the green bubble problem, but it does make WhatsApp feel less like an Android port.

The risk for Meta is that this kind of platform-specific polish costs time and money, and WhatsApp has to do it twice — once for iOS, once for Material You on Android. But the alternative is looking out of place on both platforms, and that's arguably worse. Apple isn't going to slow down its design evolution to wait for third parties to catch up.

WhatsApp's adoption of Liquid Glass is basically Meta paying rent to stay relevant on iOS, one frosted panel at a time.
