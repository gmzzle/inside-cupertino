[9to5Mac](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/09/apple-tv-shares-first-trailer-for-theres-no-place-like-home-snoopy/) shared the first trailer for *There's No Place Like Home, Snoopy*, which puts the beagle on a quest to recover his doghouse after someone at a yard sale buys it. It's exactly the kind of low-stakes, high-heart premise that's kept Peanuts specials running for decades.

Apple's Peanuts deal has been one of the quieter success stories for Apple TV+. The service grabbed exclusive streaming rights to the entire Peanuts catalog back in 2020, then started commissioning new specials and series. It was a shrewd move—nostalgic IP that parents trust, kids don't hate, and doesn't require the cultural heavy lifting of a Marvel or Star Wars. The specials consistently land in the top ten for family viewing, and Apple's added several originals since, including *The Snoopy Show* and seasonal holiday specials that actually get rewatched year after year.

The doghouse angle is smart. It's Snoopy's most iconic prop—the portal to his fantasy life as a WWI flying ace, a writer, Joe Cool. Losing it gives the special an actual emotional stake without needing to manufacture drama through new characters or convoluted plots. Peanuts works best when it's simple, and a road-trip-style recovery mission fits that mold.

This also signals Apple's doubling down on kids and family content as a retention play. It's not the flashy stuff that gets headlines, but it's the content that keeps families subscribed between *Severance* seasons. Disney's entire empire is built on that logic. Apple's catalog is still thin compared to Disney+ or Netflix, but Peanuts gives them a reliable, evergreen anchor that doesn't age out or lose relevance. Parents who grew up with Charlie Brown are now showing it to their kids on Apple TV+, which is exactly the multigenerational stickiness Apple wants.

No release date yet, but expect it timed for a school break or holiday window—Apple's learned that lesson from the Christmas and Thanksgiving specials. Snoopy's doghouse is coming home, and so are the subscribers.
