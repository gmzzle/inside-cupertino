[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/06/22/second-developer-betas-of-ios-27-macos-27-are-out?utm_source=rss) reported that Apple issued the second developer betas for iOS 27, macOS 27, and the rest of the OS lineup two weeks after WWDC. Standard cadence, nothing surprising there.

What's genuinely odd is that we're calling these iOS 27 and macOS 27 at all. Apple skipped version numbers. iOS 26 never existed. Neither did macOS 26. The company jumped straight from last year's iOS 25 to this year's 27, apparently deciding even numbers are for chumps. No official explanation, no marketing spin about lucky numbers. They just... did it.

The most likely reason? Synchronizing version numbers across platforms looked messy after years of different release schedules, and Apple wanted a clean slate. Or someone in Cupertino really likes prime numbers. Either way, it's the kind of unforced weirdness that makes you wonder who signed off on confusing every tech support forum for the next decade.

As for the betas themselves, the usual advice applies with extra emphasis: second betas are historically buggier than you'd think. Developers need to test, sure, but if you're thinking about throwing this on your daily driver because you want the new Control Center or whatever, don't. Third-party apps will break in creative ways. Battery life will be a disaster. Features Apple demoed two weeks ago might not actually work yet.

Public betas typically arrive around beta 3 or 4, sometime in July, and even those are pushing it for normal humans who need their phones to work.
