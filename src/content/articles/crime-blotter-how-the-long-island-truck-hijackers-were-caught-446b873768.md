[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/05/17/crime-blotter-how-the-long-island-truck-hijackers-were-caught?utm_source=rss) is back with another edition of their Apple crime blotter, and this week's haul is a solid reminder that criminals still haven't figured out they're carrying tracking devices.

The standout: an AirTag recovered a stolen Lamborghini. That's the dream use case Apple can't officially advertise but everyone knows exists. Someone tucked an AirTag somewhere in a six-figure car, the car got stolen, and Find My did its thing. It's the same story we've seen with bikes, backpacks, and the occasional moving truck full of someone's entire life. The difference is this time it was a Lambo, which makes for better headlines but doesn't change the fundamentals. AirTags are cheap insurance for anything you don't want walking away.

There's also movement in the Morgan McSweeney case—the young guy whose stolen iPhone became a minor social media saga. An arrest was made, which is more resolution than most phone theft cases get. McSweeney posted about his stolen phone and the saga went semi-viral, which probably didn't hurt in keeping attention on it. Most stolen iPhones just disappear into the parts-and-resale pipeline. Activation Lock has made them harder to flip intact, but that just means more of them get stripped for components. The arrest here is the exception, not the rule.

The Beyoncé stolen music case also saw a guilty plea, which is less about Apple hardware and more about someone allegedly lifting unreleased tracks. Music leaks are their own subspecies of IP theft, usually traced back to someone in the production chain who thought they could stay anonymous. They rarely can. Digital files leave trails, even when people think they've been clever.

The crime blotter is a useful reminder that Apple's ecosystem has become investigative infrastructure. AirTags find stolen stuff. Find My tracks devices. iCloud logs keep receipts. That's made life harder for opportunistic thieves and easier for people trying to recover their things—or, occasionally, their Lamborghinis.
