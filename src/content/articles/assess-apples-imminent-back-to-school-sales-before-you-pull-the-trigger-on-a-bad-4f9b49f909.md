[AppleInsider](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/07/12/assess-apples-imminent-back-to-school-sales-before-you-pull-the-trigger-on-a-bad-deal?utm_source=rss) is telling students to pump the brakes on Apple's upcoming Back to School promotion, and they're right. Apple's education sales have become predictable summer theater: modest discounts on Macs and iPads, maybe a gift card or free AirPods, all wrapped in marketing that makes it feel more generous than it is.

The reality is Apple's education pricing exists year-round—typically 10% off for students and teachers through the Education Store—and the Back to School bonus on top rarely moves the needle. Last year's promotion threw in a $150 gift card with Mac purchases, which sounds fine until you realize that gift card locks you into buying more Apple stuff at full price. It's a loyalty loop, not a deal.

What makes this year interesting is timing. The MacBook Neo just launched, and it's going to dominate student wishlists. Apple knows this. They don't need to discount aggressively when demand is already there, especially for a product that's selling well at full price. Meanwhile, Windows laptop makers are going to flood the zone with actual cash discounts—$200 off, sometimes more—to compete for that same back-to-school dollar. Dell, HP, and Lenovo don't have Apple's brand loyalty to coast on, so they discount harder.

The smarter play is waiting. Apple's education promotion will run for weeks, probably through September. Competitors' sales will overlap. If you're not in a rush, you can compare what a $150 Apple gift card actually gets you versus $200 straight off a comparable Windows machine or even a discounted previous-gen MacBook from a third-party retailer. Refurbished Macs from Apple's own store often beat the Back to School pricing, and those come with the same warranty.

Apple's education promotions work because they feel like events, not because they're exceptional deals. This year won't be different.
